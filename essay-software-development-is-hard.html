Software Development is Hard

Updated: 13 January 2014

By its nature, software is conceptual entity. It mostly deals with abstraction of real-world representations and processes. Banking software handles the representation of money in the bank account and various processes that revolve around it (deposit, withdrawal, etc). Music playing software takes care of music representation (format, quality, etc) and different ways of playing the music. The abstraction manifests itself in data structures, relationships and algorithms in the form of computer executable codes using a variety of programming languages.

This conceptual nature of software and its inherent properties make software development hard. Frederick Brooks in his classic paper, "No Silver Bullet - Essence and Accident in Software Engineering", identifies four of these properties: complexity, comformity, changeability and invisibility.

Like hardware, software consists of large number of parts; but unlike hardware, most parts in software are unique. Moreover, these parts can interact with each other in multiple ways. Combination of this and a large number of states that the software can have results in complexity. Hence, reasoning is harder; description is more tedious; modification is less reliable.

Software rarely operates alone; it has to work together with existing software and hardware; to do so its interfaces must conform. Because these interfaces are designed by different people, conformation differs between interfaces, and change from time to time. Often arbitrarily, not out of necessity.

Due to its nature, software is ever malleable. Changes are easy to make and pressure to change is ever present. This is because as it becomes useful, more people would use it and try out new use cases; its functions would need to be modified or extended. Furthermore, as its operating environment evolves (hardware and software), it must change to conform in order to stay functional. Or its business use cases have shifted, it has to change to remain competitive or become innovative.

Software is invisible; no physical representation in space that eyes can see or hands can touch. With its many parts and possible states, it's inherently hard to visualize flow of data and control, timing of activity sequence, relationship, and hierarchy. This difficulty to visualize, strip out of the mind its powerful tool to conceptualize, reason, and share with others effectively.

Apart from the nature of software itself, another contributing factor that makes software development hard is that it is mostly a creative and collaborative process. Follow on from the observation that code is design, most of the time in software development are spent in software design. Apart from technical knowledge, design activity requires creative thinking. But putting on delivery deadline on a creative process usually does not work very well. Most complex software are produced by a team of developers. Organising such intelletual collaborative effort presents its own challenges (psychological and technical).
